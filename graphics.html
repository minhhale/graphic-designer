Graphics - Turning Data Into Compelling Visuals

I create graphics that make complex stories easy to see and understand. Using tools like Adobe Creative Suite, Canva and Figma, I translate research, data, and reporting into clear layouts, thoughtful typography, and engaging visuals that guide audiences through challenging topics with clarity and impact.

Visualizing the Wang Shujun Espionage Case

Project Overview:

This graphic tells the story of Wang Shujun, a Chinese spy who infiltrated foreign networks. It condenses complex geopolitical details into a single, striking image that conveys both the human element and the national context.

Design Goals:
  • • Center Wang Shujun while framing the espionage narrative.
  • • Subtly include the Chinese flag to signal national ties.
  • • Keep the layout clear to guide the viewer’s eye.
Process:
  • • Image Selection & Composition: Selected a photo of Wang on a bench to humanize him and contrast with political symbolism.
  • • Background Integration: Added a textured Chinese flag for instant context without overpowering the subject.
  • • Visual Emphasis: Left the bench, cane, and space untouched to keep attention on Wang.
  • • Color & Texture: Used red tones and star motifs to hint at espionage and national identity.
Outcome:

The final design balances human storytelling with geopolitical tension, making it suitable for news, digital reporting, and analytical briefs.

This graphic demonstrates how thoughtful design can distill complex stories into compelling visuals that inform and engage audiences.

Uyghur Journalists’ Families Detained in China

Project Overview:

This graphic highlights the human cost of China’s campaign of repression in Xinjiang by focusing on the families of Radio Free Asia (RFA) Uyghur journalists. RFA reporters who cover human rights abuses in the region have faced intense retaliation from Chinese authorities often through the targeting of their relatives who remain inside China.

The collage depicts dozens of detained or disappeared family members, each representing a personal connection to journalists now working in exile. Their only “crime” is being related to people who report on mass detentions, religious persecution, and cultural erasure. By assembling their portraits inside the outline of Xinjiang and layering them over a dark, wall-like texture, the graphic symbolizes both the region’s isolation and the invisible prison walls separating families.

This case underscores how the Chinese government uses collective punishment to silence overseas reporting. It also illustrates the emotional toll on journalists who continue to inform the world despite threats to their loved ones.

China–Cambodia Relations Series

As part of RFA’s coverage of China–Cambodia relations, I developed a set of graphics that visually represented the growing ties between the two countries. My design challenge was to create strong, symbolic visuals that could communicate complex political and economic relationships at a glance.

Using national flags as the foundation, I explored different metaphors, such as a bowtie symbolizing partnership and blended flags suggesting unity to highlight Cambodia’s increasing alignment with China. These graphics were designed to be bold, accessible, and instantly recognizable across digital platforms.

The series effectively conveyed the nuances of Cambodia’s geopolitical position, making it easier for audiences to understand the implications of its close ties with China.
